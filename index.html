About

Stewardship, access, and community, made visible.

DoD exists to preserve and enhance mountain bike access through trail stewardship, advocacy, outreach, social rides, and responsible trail use.

Members and donors support real trail work, local partnerships, and close-to-home riding opportunities for all ages and abilities.

About / FAQ

Questions people ask first.

I'm new to the area. Where can I ride?

Wherever bikes are allowed. Locally, we have urban trails at Thurston Hills Natural Area and a growing network in the Eugene Ridgeline System. DoD is also known for work at Carpenter Bypass, also called Whypass, and for a long history supporting mountain bike trails in Oakridge.

Where can I ride in the winter without damaging trails?

Thank you for asking. Many trails are sensitive to wet-weather riding. Carpenter Bypass is designed for winter riding. Alsea Falls is often good a couple of days after rain. North Shore near Lowell is a good cross-country option. The Ridgeline system has shared-use gravel trails, and Pipedream remains open in winter.

How do I meet more riders?

Come to a Happy Ride or the Taco Tuesday ride at Thurston Hills in summer and fall. You can also introduce yourself in the Eugene Mountain Bike Facebook group to find riders with similar pace and preferences. Check the calendar or sign up for the newsletter for group rides and trail work parties.

Why are you called Disciples of Dirt?

Because we love dirt and spreading the good gospel of trail stewardship.

Why should I join DoD?

Your membership helps DoD build and maintain more local mountain bike trails. Members also get discounts at local bike shops like Lifecycle and Bicycle Way of Life. Considering how much we all spend on gas and bikes, $30 to support local trails goes a long way.

Can I donate more than the membership fee?

Of course. DoD is a registered 501(c)(3) nonprofit, so your donation is tax-deductible.

Why not just let taxes pay for trail work?

DoD supports public investment in trails, but we do not set tax rates. We advocate for bike taxes collected on mountain bikes to go toward building bike trails instead of being routed elsewhere.

I don't know anything about trail work. Can I help?

Yes. Every volunteer starts with zero experience. You will learn quickly from more seasoned trail stewards.

I want to build a mountain bike trail in my neighborhood park. Can DoD help?

Probably. First, contact the local land manager to ask about park adoption and whether other groups already maintain the area. Trail building takes longer than most people expect, but DoD has helped build and adopt several local trails.

Which local bike shop should I go to?

DoD loves all our local shops. Lifecycle carries Kona, Juliana, Norco, Pivot, Rocky Mountain, Santa Cruz, Transition, and Yeti. Bicycle Way of Life carries Trek and Scott. Hutch's carries Giant and Specialized. Show your DoD membership card for discounts on parts and accessories.

When will Eugene get more mountain bike trails?

Suzanne Arlie Park is slated for development. Eugene Parks and Open Space won a $1.2 million grant, matched with $800,000 from bond funds. Contracting has closed, and the project is required to be completed by May 2026.

I'm a beginner. How do I get started mountain biking?

Have a bike and a helmet? Go have fun. Work on basic skills by messing around on curbs, start small if you are learning to jump, and progress slowly. Knee and elbow pads are a good idea because crashing is part of the sport. Skills clinics from Ninja MTB and Dennis Silvia-Young can help, and pump tracks in Creswell, Oakridge, and Horse Creek Lodge are great for learning cornering and pumping.

Why isn't DoD part of IMBA?

IMBA does great work nationally, but many benefits they used to offer local clubs, like nonprofit administration support, have declined. For DoD, the membership fee no longer made sense for the limited local benefit.

My kid wants to mountain bike. How do I get them started?

Bring them to one of DoD's kids rides. If they are middle- or high-school-aged, check out the Eugene Composite NICA Team. They practice twice a week from summer through mid-fall and race in five events across Oregon.

What DoD does

Build, maintain, advocate, gather.

From Oakridge and Carpenter Bypass to Thurston Hills, Ridgeline conversations, and future Eugene/Springfield trail systems, DoD's role is to keep mountain biking visible, responsible, and organized.

  • Coordinate volunteer trail work and report hours.
  • Partner with land managers on access and maintenance.
  • Support events that bring riders into the club.
  • Help new riders learn where, when, and how to ride responsibly.
